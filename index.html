State of the City

A look at four major indicators of the quality of life in New York City

In a city of five distinct boroughs, hundreds neighborhoods, and millions of people with vastly backgrounds and living situations, it’s incredibly hard for people living inside and outside the area to get a sense of what direction the city is moving in. Below is a look at how the economy, housing, education, and crime effect the residents of New York City.

Economy

The Great Recession hit the New York area job market heavily, especially in boroughs like the Bronx and Brooklyn. However, it did little to stem the upperward trend of consumer prices. Since the recession, consumers across the United States have felt an increased squeeze on their wallets as prices have risen at a faster pace than hiring. Urban centers like New York City are no exception.

Housing

Since the end of the Great Recession, there has been an uneven response across the boroughs in terms of the housing market. Manhattan and Brooklyn sale prices have skyrocketed, while the rest of the boroughs have been stagnant or only seen moderate gains. One consistent theme across the city has been rental prices. All five boroughs have seen a steady increase in median rents since 2012. This increase has fed what many would call a homeless crisis in city. The homeless population, which has bounced between 20,000 and 40,000 since the mid 1980s, has now jumped to over 60,000.

Education

Progress in New York's education system is difficult to track due to changes in testing and graduation standards. Since the New York State graduation rate method was adopted in 2005, New York City has seen a significant increase in its four-year graduation rate. Much of this growth is due to the upswing in students getting Regents diplomas. The last revision to the New York State Common Core Learning Standards Test standards was in 2013. Since then, New York City has seen steady increases in the percentage of students receiving satisfactory scores. However, scores still vary significantly between schools.

Crime

Since 2000, overall crime in New York City has steadily decreased. Most of this drop has been driven by reductions in crimes likes robbery, burglary, and grand larceny auto. Violent crime like murder, rape, and felony assault have seen a much slower drop and in some cases recent upticks.
